What we learned → What comes next

Light (r = +0.55) is the strongest cycle-level driver: brighter cycles dry faster. Humidity (r = −0.37) is negative because wetter air slows drying. But correlation isn't mechanism. To build a real model, we need to understand WHY, and specifically separate the biological signal (the plant opening and closing stomata) from the purely physical evaporation that would happen even without a plant. That split determines which features belong in our model.

STEP 03BIOLOGICAL FACTORS

What the plant is doing

How does plant physiology drive soil moisture loss?

Plants aren't passive. They actively regulate water loss through their stomata, tiny pores on leaves that open for photosynthesis and close to conserve water. Stomata are fully open around midday and shut at night. Every biological effect below is measured directly from sensor data.

During daylight, plants open their stomata to absorb CO₂ for photosynthesis. Water evaporates through these open pores as an unavoidable side effect. This process, called transpiration, accounts for the majority of daytime water loss. At night, stomata close to conserve water, so the drying rate drops dramatically.

As soil dries, the plant reduces transpiration (ABA stress response)
First quarter vs. last quarter of each drying cycle | —
When soil dries below a critical threshold, plant roots produce ABA (abscisic acid). This stress hormone signals leaf stomata to partially close, reducing water loss. Our sensor detects this: the drying rate systematically decreases in the second half of each cycle compared to the first.
What we learned → What comes next

The plant's biology is clear: stomata open at 13:00 (peak drying), ABA stress reduces late-cycle transpiration by ~21%, and the circadian FFT peak sits exactly at 24 h. But biology alone doesn't tell the whole story. The same day/night pattern also has a physical origin: temperature, humidity, and substrate diffusion physics all operate on a 24-hour cycle too.

STEP 04PHYSICAL FACTORS

What the environment is doing

What physical mechanisms drive water loss independently of the plant?

Even if the plant were removed, the soil would still lose water. Temperature, air dryness (VPD), and substrate physics drive evaporation regardless of biology. Fickian diffusion predicts soil drying follows an exponential law: moisture ∝ 1 − e−t/τ, where τ is the time constant.

Near-dry soil (ADC 600–800) dries faster than freshly watered soil. This is a time-of-day selection effect: dry soil values occur during active transpiration hours, while wet values occur at night.
What we learned → What comes next

Physics confirms: VPD strongly correlates with drying rate (r=0.58 cycle-level), temperature adds an independent contribution (r=0.32), and drying follows a consistent linear trend across all 11 cycles. We now have two independent mechanistic explanations for the same pattern: biological (stomata) and physical (evaporation, VPD). The next step is to combine them into a single quantitative model.

STEP 05PUTTING IT TOGETHER

From observations to model inputs

What did Steps 03 and 04 actually find, and how does each finding become a model feature?

Steps 03 and 04 weren't just exploratory. Every finding produced a quantified, justified driver of soil drying. Those drivers become the five features of the prediction model. This step makes that connection explicit.

Stomata rhythm
Step 03: Biological
When stomata open at sunrise, drying rate jumps 7×: from 1.9 ADC/h at night to 13.1 ADC/h during the day. This is the single largest driver of water loss.
Welch t-test p < 0.001 · n = 11 cycles → Feature: stomata(h) × light/4095
Air dryness (VPD)
Step 04: Physical
Dry air pulls 6× more water from the soil than humid air. VPD, which combines temperature and humidity into a single evaporation index, is the strongest cycle-scale predictor.
Cycle-level r = 0.58 · derived from sensor T & RH → Feature: sat_vp(T) · (1 − RH/100)
Light intensity
Step 04: Physical
Direct sunlight drives 19× more evaporation than complete darkness, independently of stomata. Radiation heats the soil surface and drives bare-soil water loss even when the plant is inactive.
Cycle-level r = 0.55 · 0h causal lag confirmed → Feature: light / 4095
ABA stress closure
Step 03: Biological
As the soil dries, the plant progressively closes its stomata, reducing the drying rate by 21% by the end of a cycle. This self-limiting behaviour must be accounted for or the model overestimates late-cycle drying.
Early-cycle vs late-cycle rate · measured across all 11 cycles → Feature: φ = (soil − 280) / (800 − 280)
Temperature
Step 04: Physical
Higher temperature increases kinetic energy of water molecules, boosting evaporation rate. Weaker than VPD at the cycle scale (r = 0.32) but adds independent variance and is already measured by the sensor.
Cycle-level r = 0.32 · partially captured by VPD already → Feature: temp (°C)
What we found → What we build

Five empirically justified features. Four measured by the sensor directly (light, temp, humidity), one derived (VPD), two engineered from first principles (stomata×light interaction, cycle phase). Each feature has a known sign and physical meaning. The next step: fit a Ridge regression to those features, evaluate it under Leave-One-Out cross-validation, and translate the output into a watering schedule.

STEP 06THE MODEL

Predicting when to water without a soil sensor

Can environmental data alone replace per-plant soil sensing?

Steps 03–05 identified every major driver. Now those drivers become model features. A Ridge regression predicts the hourly soil drying rate from environmental inputs. Integrating that rate over time gives a full soil moisture trajectory — and the moment it crosses the dry threshold is our predicted watering time. No soil sensor needed at deployment.

Reading the equation: Stomata×Light (β=+19.74) and light alone (β=+31.44) dominate, confirming that radiation drives most water loss. The large negative intercept (−12.30) represents the dark-night baseline when all drivers are near zero. Cycle phase (β=+14.77) captures the ABA stress-induced stomata closure: as the soil dries, the phase term grows, but the closing stomata reduce transpiration, creating the self-limiting drying curve observed in Step 04.
